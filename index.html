Stage 2 — Weighted score

Scoring anchors — what each score means

Score every company against these anchors before applying weights. Anchors are set before research begins so scores don't drift based on the order you look at companies.

Market size
1–3 · Niche
Geographically or demographically constrained. Unlikely to support a Series A valuation without significant expansion assumptions.
4–6 · Real but bounded
Meaningful market in one country or segment. Credible path to scale but requires expansion to justify venture returns.
7–10 · Large & addressable
Even 1% penetration produces a large business. Think payments infrastructure across 4+ markets, or SME lending in Egypt where 90%+ of businesses are underserved.
Traction efficiency — users or customers ÷ total capital raised
1–3 · Bottom third
High capital deployed relative to traction achieved. Rank all companies by this ratio — bottom third score here.
4–7 · Middle third
Reasonable efficiency. Capital deployment and traction broadly in line with peers in the database.
8–10 · Top third
Doing a lot with a little. Traction clearly outpaces capital deployed relative to peers. Score this mechanically — it's the most objective criterion.
Founder-market fit — assessed via LinkedIn
1–3 · Generalist
No obvious connection to the problem, sector, or geography. Could be executing any startup.
4–6 · Adjacent
Fintech in a different market, or relevant sector in the same geography. Credible but not an obvious insider.
7–10 · Earned insight
Previously worked in financial services in this specific market, or built and exited a relevant company. Has regulatory relationships or distribution advantages others don't.
Competitive moat
1–3 · Replicable
Product could be copied in 6 months with enough capital. No evidence of structural advantage. Default score if no public evidence found: 3.
4–6 · Soft advantage
Technical lead or brand that could erode with sufficient competitor capital. Takes time to replicate but not structurally protected.
7–10 · Structural
Named, verifiable regulatory licence, proprietary data asset, or deeply embedded distribution partnership. Something that takes years to build.
Syndicate quality — scored mechanically against the qualifying investors list
1–3 · No qualifying investors
No investors from the defined qualifying list. Generalist or non-Africa-specialist backers only.
4–7 · One qualifying investor
One qualifying investor present. Score 5–6 if not leading, 7 if leading the round.
8–10 · Strong syndicate
Two or more qualifying investors, with at least one leading. Strong signal of ecosystem credibility and Series A pathway.
Definition: what makes a syndicate "strong"

A strong syndicate in African fintech is one that brings more than capital. The core test: could this investor group meaningfully improve the company's odds of reaching Series A and beyond?


Four criteria must be met: (1) at least one investor with prior Africa-specific fintech portfolio companies, demonstrating they understand local regulatory environments; (2) demonstrated ability to participate in or facilitate a Series A, either through fund size or established relationships with Series A investors; (3) evidence of follow-on investment in winning portfolio companies; (4) network value in the relevant geography — relationships with central banks, distribution partners, or talent that the company can actually access.


Investors are scored on these four criteria (1–10 each) and tiered by average: Tier 1 ≥ 7.5, Tier 2 = 5.0–7.4, Tier 3 < 5.0. A strong syndicate requires at least one Tier 1 investor, or two or more Tier 2 investors with an average score above 6.5. Syndicate quality scores in the database reflect this logic directly.
